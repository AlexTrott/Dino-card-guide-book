CPU AI Secrets

How the CPU Thinks

By reverse-engineering the cpuChooseMove() function from DinoCard's source code, we can reveal exactly how the CPU opponent decides its next move. The AI is not random -- it follows a weighted probability system based on three key factors.

Factor 1: Reserve Energy. The CPU checks how much energy it currently has. At 0 reserves, it is forced to Hold. At 1 reserve, it can only Strike or Guard. With 2 or more reserves, all four moves become available. Energy is the hard constraint that limits everything else.

Factor 2: HP Percentage. The CPU calculates its current HP as a fraction of its maximum HP. This determines its overall behavioral posture. Above 50% HP, the CPU plays aggressively. Between 25% and 50%, it shifts toward defensive play. Below 25%, it enters a desperate mode that swings between all-in attacks and full defense.

Factor 3: The Player's Last Move. The CPU remembers what you did last turn and adjusts its probability weights accordingly. If you used Power Strike, it expects another heavy hit and favors Guard. If you used Guard, it sees an opening and favors aggression. This reactive behavior is the key to exploiting the AI.

Key Insight: The CPU Is Predictable

The CPU never truly randomizes. It picks from weighted probability tables that shift based on the three factors above. Once you understand these tables, you can manipulate the CPU into making the moves you want it to make. Every move you choose is not just an action -- it is bait for the CPU's next turn.

At 0 Reserves: The CPU must Hold. There is no decision to make. This is the most exploitable state -- you know with 100% certainty what it will do. Always Power Strike a CPU that has 0 energy.

At 1 Reserve: The CPU can only choose between Strike (cost 1) and Guard (cost 1). Power Strike and Hold are off the table. The probabilities shift based on your last move, but the CPU is heavily constrained. It cannot threaten you with Power Strike, so you can safely skip Guard.

At 2+ Reserves: All four moves are available, and the CPU's HP percentage becomes the dominant factor. This is where the full probability tables kick in, and where reading the AI becomes both possible and profitable.

Pro Tip

The CPU evaluates these factors in order: reserves first, then HP, then your last move. This means energy constraints always override behavioral tendencies. A desperate low-HP CPU that wants to Guard cannot do so if it has 0 reserves -- it must Hold instead.

Exploiting the AI

Knowing the probability tables is useful, but turning that knowledge into consistent wins requires specific strategies. The following five techniques exploit the CPU's predictable reactions to manipulate it into losing positions.

1. The Guard Bait

Use Guard repeatedly. The CPU interprets Guard as a "safe" or passive play and responds overwhelmingly with Strike -- 80% of the time at 1 reserve, and up to 40% Power Strike at 2+ reserves when it has high HP. After two or three Guards in a row, the CPU is almost certainly going to Strike.

Once the pattern is established, switch to your own Power Strike on the turn the CPU commits to Strike. Power Strike ignores Guard, so you get the full 1.5x damage multiplier against an unguarded opponent. The CPU walked right into it.

Guard » Guard » Guard » Power Strike!
Tip

Guard Bait works best when the CPU has 1 reserve, where its Strike probability after seeing Guard reaches 80%. At 2+ reserves with high HP, the CPU may throw Power Strike (40%) instead of Strike, which still benefits you since your Guard reduces Strike damage but does nothing against Power Strike. Adjust timing accordingly.

2. The Power Strike Scare

Open the battle with a Power Strike. The CPU reacts to Power Strike by heavily favoring Guard on its next turn -- 40% at high HP, 50% at mid HP, and a massive 60% at low HP. Guard costs 1 energy, so the CPU is burning reserves to protect itself from a follow-up Power Strike that never comes.

After your opening Power Strike, switch to regular Strikes. The CPU is wasting energy on Guard while you chip away with efficient 1-cost attacks. When the CPU's reserves finally drop to 0 and it is forced to Hold, unleash another Power Strike for devastating free damage.

Power Strike » Strike » Strike » Strike » Power Strike (on 0 reserves)

3. The Hold Trap

When the CPU drops below 25% HP, it enters desperate mode and becomes extremely defensive -- 50% to 60% Guard probability after seeing your Strike or Power Strike. Use Hold during this phase. Hold costs nothing, so you are regenerating energy for free while the CPU wastes its reserves on Guard after Guard.

The CPU sees your Hold and might try to Strike (20-30% chance), but even if it does, you are stockpiling energy. Once the CPU has drained its reserves to 0 and must Hold, drop a Power Strike to finish the fight.

Hold (free) » Hold (free) » CPU drains reserves » Power Strike!
Warning

The Hold Trap only works when the CPU is below 25% HP. At higher HP, the CPU plays more aggressively and is much more likely to punish your Hold with Strike or Power Strike. Do not attempt this strategy against a healthy CPU.

4. The Aggro Rush

Against a CPU at high HP (above 50%) with 2 or more reserves, alternate between Strike and Power Strike. The CPU in this state plays a fairly balanced game, but critically it almost never Holds -- only 10% to 20% of the time. This means your attacks will almost always deal full damage because the CPU is not regenerating or building reserves defensively.

The key insight is that the high-HP CPU tries to fight back rather than play safe. It trades blows with you instead of turtling. If your dino has superior ATK stats, you win this trade war. Push the aggro advantage before the CPU's HP drops low enough to trigger its defensive modes.

Strike » Power Strike » Strike » Power Strike
Tip

The Aggro Rush burns through your energy fast. Make sure you have enough reserves to sustain the assault, or mix in a Hold between attacks to refuel. The goal is to drop the CPU below 50% HP before you run out of steam.

5. Energy Counting

This is not a single strategy but a discipline that amplifies every other strategy. Track the CPU's reserve energy throughout the battle. Every move it makes has a known cost: Strike costs 1, Guard costs 1, Power Strike costs 2, and Hold is free (and regenerates 1). By counting, you always know the CPU's constraints.

If the CPU used Power Strike last turn and had 2 energy, it now has 0. It must Hold. That is a guaranteed free Power Strike for you. If it had 3 energy and used Power Strike, it now has 1 -- it can only Strike or Guard, never Power Strike. You can safely skip Guard yourself because the worst it can do is a regular Strike.

Common Mistake

Many players forget that the CPU regenerates 1 energy when it Holds. If the CPU had 0 reserves and Held, it now has 1 -- not 0. Always account for Hold regeneration when counting. Also remember that the CPU's starting reserves vary by dino card, so note the initial value at the start of each battle.
